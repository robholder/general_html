Background Plate Node

The Background Plate node is used to integrate elements with a background plate - providing passes to influence the integration of your 3D element with an image or video.

Background stand-in geometry

To utilize the Background Plate, you need some stand-in or proxy geometry to act as a shadow catcher. The node provides more than this, but that's the principle of how it works. It stands as a CG proxy to catch influences from the 3d scene.

The most common proxy would be a ground plane, but you can get quite sophisticated if you need more complex elements.

This geometry (along with the geometry elements you want to integrate and render) can be built in the Obj context.

Camera

In the 'Obj' context, you can add your camera as normal. (This could be a static or tracked setup.)

Add your 'background' image (back plate) into the camera:

  • Camera parameters:
    • Background Image field:
      • Add the path to the image or sequence of images.

Background Plate

After the lights:

  • Add a Background Plate node:
    • We need to populate the Primitives field with a list of all the geometry which is acting as shadow catcer geometry.
    • You can open the Scene Graph window and drag the items from the list to the field.
    • These items will virtually disappear in the viewport at this stage:
      • (If you were to remove the Stage camera's background image, you would see the ghost of the items against the grid.)
      • These itmes are now a matte to the regular render.
    • Add the background plate to the Background Plate node's parameters:
      • In the Background section:
        • Plate field:
          • Add the path to the background image.
          • This will NOT be visible in the Viewport - it's just added to the data of the Background Plate node.

Add the Karma Renderer

After the Background Plate node, we need to drop a Karma setup.

  • Tab > Karma:
    • Plug both outputs from the Background Plate node into the Karma Render Settings node.
    • The second input is for additional render AOVs
  • On the Image Output tab of the Karma Render Settings node parameters:
    • Check 'On' Import Render Vars From Second Input
    • All the information will now be passed to the renderer.

Check the camera resolution matches the background image (resolution or aspect ratio at least)

Emission

You can also include emission from a CG element (to influence the proxy geometry for compositing into the backplate).

  • Imaging an element (Sphere perhaps) sitting on a proxy ground plane.
    • Add an 'emissive' material.
    • Off the bat, it'll affect any other CG elements as you'd expect - casting light onto them.
  • To activate influence on the proxy geometry:
    • In the Background Plate node's parameters:
      • Holdout AOVs section:
        • Check 'On' the Emission checkbox.

Glossy Reflections and Occlusion

These options are also available on the Background Plate (Holdout AOVs section).

To see the layers in the Viewport

On the right-most icon bar of the Viewport, there's an icon that resembles a stack of layers - click this to select which AOV to interactively render in the Viewport (with the KarmaCPU or KarmaXPU render option).

There are various AOVs depending on your setup, but the first few are from the Background Plate node's data:

  • C - rendered file (color or beauty)
  • holdout_diffuse
  • holdout_shadows (an inverted version of the shadows cast onto the proxy geometry - can be split by light tag)
  • holdout_reflection_occlusion
  • holdout_glossy_reflection
  • holdout_emission

Briefing Document: Houdini Background Plate LOP

Date: October 26, 2023 Source: "Mardini 2024 | Day 9 | Background Plate" (Video Transcript) Purpose: To understand the functionality and use of the Background Plate LOP in Houdini for integrating CG elements into live-action footage.

I. Core Concept: Integrating CG with Live-Action Plates

The primary purpose of the Background Plate LOP (Light Operation) is to seamlessly combine CG elements with a pre-existing background plate (live-action footage, for instance). This is achieved by using the background plate as a canvas and the CG geometry as actors within that canvas, catching shadows and reflections from other elements. The focus is on making the CG additions look as if they exist naturally within the filmed environment.

II. Key Workflow Steps

Geometry Recreation

  • Purpose: To create a rough 3D model of the key objects within the background plate (e.g., a table, other props).
  • Method: Using basic shapes in Houdini, the geometry is matched by eye, or through more precise object or camera tracking workflows, to the existing background plate.
  • Quote: "…you would want to take some time you might even use a third party application which can do object tracking and Camera tracking"

Material Application:

  • Purpose: To define how the geometry will interact with light, i.e., apply the required materials such as glossy or emission.

Background Plate Setup:

  • Purpose: To load the actual background plate image into Houdini.
  • Method: Using the Camera's background image settings, the reference plate is loaded, allowing visualization and alignment of the CG elements. The view option can also be set to hidden line invisible, for a see-through overlay of the geometry.

Background Plate LOP Usage:

  • Purpose: The heart of this workflow, the Background Plate LOP handles the complex task of creating hold-out mattes, casting shadows, and integrating light and reflections, and rendering.
  • "Matte Objects": Specific 3D geometry (table, candles, etc.) is designated as "matte objects." These objects are not directly rendered, but act as "hold-outs", blocking other elements, and capturing shadows and reflections.
  • Quote: "…when I say matte objects these are going to be your hold out objects so it's going to be our table, it's going to be this geometry in the middle and it's going to be that candle over there..."

Camera Settings & Resolution:

  • Purpose: Ensure the render matches the background plate resolution.
  • Method: Setting the camera and render resolution to match that of the background plate (e.g. 1080 x 1920).

Karma Render Settings:

  • Purpose: To initiate the rendering process
  • Method: A comma render settings node is added, the first input uses our stage data, and the second input uses additional render vars (AOV's, additional render layers). We use the image output parameter on the comma render settings to import render layers from the second input
  • Quote: "… the second input is actually where the render settings are stored this first one is just our geometry plus the plate so let's just drag this over here and then we need to tell this node to actually use the second input for our render vs..."

Background Plate Integration (Compositing Layers):

  • Purpose: Render out specific layers for compositing
  • Method: The LOP outputs several layers including hold out shadows, reflections and diffusion
  • Quote: "…we have this option over here for render outputs we can see our hold out Shadows just like that so this is an inverted version of our shadow layer..."
  • Compositing Formula: In compositing, the final result is obtained through specific layering and blending. As the video explains, an example of this is: (Background Plate * Inverted Hold-out Shadows) + Hold-out Reflection Occlusion + Hold-out Diffusion + Hold-out Emission + Hold-out Glassy Reflection

Direct Render (Optional):

  • Purpose: To render a directly composited final render
  • Method: By adding a render settings edit node after the comma render settings and enabling "use background", the scene is rendered with background plate integration and all layers baked-in.
  • Quote: "…where it says use background we're going to say set or create and just set this to on we're telling it now to actually render this background into our final render"
Emission:
  • Purpose: The Background Plate LOP allows for light emitted from CG objects to interact with background plate elements
  • Method: When the 'emission' parameter is enabled on the Background Plate LOP, emissive objects in the CG will illuminate the background plate in the render.
  • Quote: "...if we wanted to affect our actual plate then what we need to do is just go down here to our background plate and enable emission right you'll see that it now looks as if it's lighting up the things that are part of the background plate..."

III. Key Takeaways

  • Flexibility: The Background Plate LOP is versatile, supporting shadows, reflections, and emission effects on background plates
  • Layered Approach: The process often involves generating separate render passes/AOV's, particularly for compositing.
  • Precise Geometry Matching is Important: Although the example uses a rough estimation, more precise geometry can improve the quality and realism of the composited result.
  • Node Complexity: It's a complex node, requiring proper understanding of all parameters and workflows to produce intended results.

IV. Summary

The Background Plate LOP in Houdini is a powerful tool for compositing CG elements into existing live-action footage. By using matte objects, capturing shadows and reflections, and enabling emission, the LOP allows artists to integrate CG elements into existing footage seamlessly, and is best done via a multi layered compositing approach for the best results. It is also possible to render out the final composite directly within Houdini if desired, greatly simplifying the workflow.

FAQs

FAQ: Using the Background Plate LOP in Houdini

What is the primary purpose of the Background Plate LOP node?

The Background Plate LOP is primarily used for integrating CG elements (like a rendered 3D object) into an existing background plate (such as a photograph or video footage). It facilitates adding effects like shadows, reflections, and emission from your CG elements to the background plate, making it appear as though these elements are actually part of the original scene. It works by treating specific geometries as matte objects, allowing them to interact with lighting and other elements without being directly rendered.

How do I set up the geometry for use with the Background Plate LOP?

You first need to create a rough 3D model of the existing elements in the background plate, such as a table or other objects. This doesn't have to be perfectly accurate, but it needs to be good enough to cast and receive shadows or other effects correctly. You match this geometry up with the background plate, often using a frame of the footage as a background image in your viewport. This can be done using manual alignment or more advanced techniques like object or camera tracking.

What are "matte objects" in the context of the Background Plate LOP, and how are they used?

Matte objects, also known as "holdout" objects, are the 3D geometries that you created to represent the scene's existing elements. In the Background Plate LOP, these are selected under "Primitives" and treated as invisible surfaces that catch shadows, reflections, and emission from CG elements without being rendered themselves. They act as occluders, blocking the visibility of any CG object that would be behind them and receive shadows as if they were in the physical scene. This creates the effect of seamless integration of CG objects into the background.

How do I specify which background plate to use in the Background Plate LOP?

To specify the background plate, you need to select the image or video under "Background Plate" within the Background Plate LOP node's parameters. This image will be used by the node to composite the CG elements with and is necessary for a proper effect. The background plate will not appear unless used with render setting, and then added in as a background.

Why do I need a Karma render settings node, and how does it interact with the Background Plate LOP?

The Karma render settings node is essential to render out the results of the Background Plate LOP. The Background Plate LOP outputs geometry and a set of render variables; these are then connected to the first and second inputs of the Karma render settings node respectively. The render settings node processes the scene geometry and applies the data from the Background Plate LOP. By using the second input, which receives the additional "render vars" or AOV's (arbitrary output variables), the shadows, reflections and other effects captured are applied.

How do I render the final composited output directly from Houdini?

To render a fully composited output directly from Houdini including the background plate, a Render Settings Edit node is used. After your Karma render settings, place this node and under the "comma" tab, then under global settings, set "use background" to "on." This tells Houdini to render the composited image, taking into account the shadows, reflections, emission and other layers created by the Background Plate LOP and render them directly onto the background plate to get a final image.

Beyond shadows, what other effects can the Background Plate LOP handle?

Besides shadows, the Background Plate LOP can also handle emission and reflections. By enabling the "emission" option on the Background Plate LOP, any emissive materials applied to CG elements will affect the lighting of the background plate as if it were a real surface within the scene. This way, glowing objects can illuminate parts of the background image. Reflections can also be captured and applied to make the composited image much more convincing.

If I don't want a single composited image, how do I get the individual render layers (like just the shadow pass)?

To output the render layers individually, you can disable the "use background" option in the Render Settings Edit node, allowing you to output the shadow pass, diffuse pass, and other AOV’s generated by the Background Plate LOP separately. Then, these layers can be manually composited in a different program (such as Houdini's compositing context or another compositing software). The network provided with the Background Plate LOP documentation shows how to recombine all of these layers into a final image.

Quiz

What is the primary function of the Background Plate LOP node in Houdini?

The Background Plate LOP node is primarily used to integrate VFX elements with an existing background plate. It allows users to add CGI objects into a scene while interacting with the background's shadows, reflections, and emissions.

How do you create a rough estimate of the geometry of the background plate for this node to work?

To create a rough estimate of the background plate geometry, a single frame is screen-captured and used as a background image in the camera. Then, 3D geometry (like a table and objects) are modeled roughly matching the image.

What are "matte objects" and how do you define them in the Background Plate LOP?

Matte objects, in this context, are the 3D objects in the scene that are not directly rendered but act as "hold-outs." They catch shadows, reflections, and emissions. In the Background Plate LOP, these objects are defined by selecting them and dragging them to the "Primitives" input.

Why do you need a Karma Render Settings node and how is it connected to the background plate?

The Karma Render Settings node is needed to specify the render resolution and to fetch the necessary render information from the Background Plate LOP. The first input connects to the geometry and background plate data, while the second input carries additional render variables to enable correct rendering.

What does "import render from second input" do within the Karma Render Settings node?

"Import render from second input" instructs the render settings to use the render variables passed through the second input. These variables are how things like the shadows get added to the background plate.

How does the hold out shadow render output work?

The hold out shadow output shows an inverted version of the shadows cast by the matte objects. The bright areas indicate where the most shadows are being cast, allowing users to see the areas where shadows are affecting the background plate.

What is the purpose of a Render Settings Edit node and how does it help render the composite?

The Render Settings Edit node is used to enable the rendering of the background plate directly in the output. By turning "use background" to "on" in the node, a fully composite render that combines background and VFX elements is produced.

How can the Background Plate LOP simulate emission on the background plate?

The Background Plate LOP can simulate emission by enabling the "emission" option in the node, then by adding an emissive material to a CGI object. The emission will affect the background plate, just like shadows would.

Summarize the required steps for setting up the Background Plate LOP workflow.

First, you define matte objects that cast/receive shadows, then select the background plate, set the render variables to “import render from second input”, add the karma render settings node, and potentially use the render settings edit node to turn on the background in your render.

What happens when "use background" is disabled within the Render Settings Edit node?

If "use background" is disabled in the Render Settings Edit node, the final render will not include the background plate, and instead you would only output the various render layers, including shadow, reflection, emission, and diffuse layers, for compositing.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • LOP (Light Operations): In Houdini, a LOP is a node used within the Solaris context to manage scene description and rendering operations. They deal with scene objects, cameras, materials, and lights for rendering.
  • Background Plate: In VFX, a background plate is a pre-existing video or image of a real-world scene that CGI elements are composited into.
  • Matte Objects (Hold-Out Objects): 3D geometry used to mask or reveal elements in a render. These objects are not rendered but act as "shadow catchers" or "reflectors", affecting how other elements interact with the background plate.
  • Karma Render Settings: A Houdini node used to control the various aspects of a Karma render, such as image resolution and render variables (AOVs) for compositing.
  • AOV (Arbitrary Output Variables): Render passes or channels that provide specific types of information about the render, such as diffuse, reflection, shadows, or depth. Also called “render vars.”
  • Render Settings Edit: A Houdini node that allows for the modification of render settings just before rendering, such as turning on the background plate during composited renders.
  • Emission: The process of an object emitting light, simulated within a 3D environment to illuminate its surroundings.
  • Primitive: The basic building blocks of geometry in Houdini, such as points, lines, polygons, and volumes, used to construct the scene.
  • Composite: The process of combining multiple images or layers into a single final image.
  • Hidden Line Invisible: A display mode in Houdini that shows only the wireframe structure of geometry, making it easier to match geometry to the background image.
  • Screen Capture: The act of taking a still image of what is currently displayed on the monitor.
