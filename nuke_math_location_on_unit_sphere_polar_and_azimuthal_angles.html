Point on a Unit Sphere Given the Polar Angle and the Azimuth Angle

Coordinate System

In the standard mathematical and physics context from which these formulas are derived, and in many 3D graphics applications:

  • The y-axis is synonymous with "up and down" ⬆️.
  • The z-axis is synonymous with "toward and away from the viewer" (or depth) ↔️.

This is often referred to as a right-handed coordinate system. If you point the thumb of your right hand along the positive x-axis, your index finger along the positive y-axis, and your middle finger along the positive z-axis, they form a perpendicular "L" shape, with the z-axis pointing straight out.

The formulas below are used to find a point on a sphere of radius r using two angles:

  • ϕ (phi), the inclination or polar angle from the positive z-axis,
  • θ (theta), the azimuthal angle from the positive x-axis in the xy-plane.

The standard formulas are:

  • x = r * sin(ϕ) * cos(θ)
  • y = r * sin(ϕ) * sin(θ)
  • z = r * cos(ϕ)

For a unit sphere, where radius r=1. The formulas simplify to:

  • x = sin(ϕ) * cos(θ)
  • y = sin(ϕ) * sin(θ)
  • z = cos(ϕ)

The Common Orientation for Computer Graphics

This system is often used in computer graphics and game development.

  • x = cos(rotation.y) * cos(rotation.x)
  • y = sin(rotation.x)
  • z = sin(rotation.y) * cos(rotation.x)

How it Works

The formula provided above is a variation of the standard spherical coordinates. It uses two angles: one for horizontal rotation (rotation.y) and another for vertical rotation (rotation.x).

  • Vertical Rotation (rotation.x): This angle, which we can call ϕ, controls the "up and down" movement.
    • The y-coordinate is determined by sin(ϕ). When ϕ is 0, the point is on the equator (y=0). When ϕ is 90º, it's at the north pole (y=1).
    • The term cos(ϕ) acts as a scaling factor for the x and z coordinates. When you're at the equator (ϕ=0), cos(ϕ)=1, so the x and z values are at their full range. As you move toward the poles (ϕ→±90º), cos(ϕ) → 0, which correctly shrinks the x and z values to zero, bringing the point to the pole.
  • Horizontal Rotation (rotation.y): This angle, which we can call θ, controls the "side-to-side" movement around the y-axis.
    • The x-coordinate is determined by cos(θ) and the scaling factor from the vertical rotation.
    • The z-coordinate is determined by sin(θ) and the same scaling factor.

This setup is consistent and works because it's a direct geometric projection. It's as if you first rotate a point around the x-axis and then rotate the resulting point around the y-axis.

Comparing to the Standard Formula

The formula above effectively re-assigns the axes and angles compared to the standard mathematical convention:

Feature Graphics Formula Standard Formula
"Up/Down" Axis y z
"Toward/Away" Axis z y
Vertical Angle rotation.x ϕ (Polar Angle)
Horizontal Angle rotation.y θ (Azimuthal Angle)

This is why the terms appear in different places but still produce a valid result on the sphere. It's a matter of coordinate system and angle naming convention

How the Correct Formulas Work 🌐

The derivation of these formulas relies on a two-step application of basic trigonometry.

Imagine a point P on the unit sphere.

Finding the z-coordinate:

  • The polar angle ϕ is the angle between the positive z-axis and the line segment from the origin to point P.
  • This creates a right-angled triangle where the hypotenuse is the radius of the sphere (which is 1 for a unit sphere). The adjacent side to the angle ϕ is the vertical distance from the origin, which is the z-coordinate.
  • Using the definition of cosine (cos(ϕ)= hypotenuse/adjacent) we get:
    • cos(ϕ)= z/1 = z = cos(ϕ)

Finding the x and y-coordinates:

  • The side opposite to the angle ϕ is the radius of a circle created by projecting the point P onto the xy-plane. Let's call this projected radius r-xy
  • Using the definition of sine (sin(ϕ)= hypotenuse/opposite), we find the length of this projected radius:

sin(ϕ)= rxy/1 = rxy =sin(ϕ)

  • Now, we have a new right-angled triangle in the xy-plane. The hypotenuse of this triangle is rxy. The azimuthal angle θ is the angle between the positive x-axis and this projected radius rxy
  • Using sine and cosine on this new triangle:
  • x = rxy cos(θ) = sin(ϕ)cos(θ)
  • y = rxy sin(θ) = sin(ϕ)sin(θ)

By combining these two steps, we can find any point on a unit sphere using two angles.

Graphic Representation

How the Graphic Representation Works

The graphic representation of this concept, whether static or interactive, typically highlights two key right-angled triangles to demonstrate the formulas.

  • The Vertical Triangle 📐: This triangle is in a vertical plane that slices the sphere. Its vertices are:
    • The origin (0,0,0).
    • The point P on the surface of the sphere.
    • The point on the z-axis directly below or above P.
    • The hypotenuse of this triangle is the radius of the sphere, which is 1 for a unit sphere.
    • The adjacent side to the polar angle ϕ is the z-coordinate.
    • The opposite side to the angle ϕ is the projected radius onto the xy-plane.

This triangle is what gives us the formulas for the z-coordinate (z=cos(ϕ)) and the projected radius (rxy=sin(ϕ)).

  • The Horizontal Triangle 🔺: This triangle is in the xy-plane. Its vertices are:
  • The origin (0,0,0).
  • The point where the projected radius intersects the x-axis.
  • The point where the projected radius intersects the y-axis. The hypotenuse of this triangle is the projected radius from the vertical triangle (rxy).
  • The adjacent side to the azimuthal angle θ is the x-coordinate.
  • The opposite side to the angle θ is the y-coordinate.

Using this triangle, we get the formulas for the x and y coordinates: x=rxy cos(θ) and y=r xy sin(θ). Since we already know that rxy = sin(ϕ), we can substitute it in to get the final formulas: x=sin(ϕ)cos(θ) and y=sin(ϕ)sin(θ).
