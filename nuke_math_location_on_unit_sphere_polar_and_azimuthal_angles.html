Coordinate System

In the standard mathematical and physics context from which these formulas are derived, and in many 3D graphics applications:

  • The y-axis is synonymous with "up and down" ‚¨ÜÔ∏è.

This is often referred to as a right-handed coordinate system. If you point the thumb of your right hand along the positive x-axis, your index finger along the positive y-axis, and your middle finger along the positive z-axis, they form a perpendicular "L" shape, with the z-axis pointing straight out.

The formulas below are used to find a point on a sphere of radius r using two angles:

  • œï (phi), the polar angle from the positive z-axis,
  • Œ∏ (theta), the azimuthal angle from the positive x-axis in the xy-plane.

The standard formulas are:

  • x=rsin(œï)cos(Œ∏)
  • y=rsin(œï)sin(Œ∏)
  • z=rcos(œï)

For a unit sphere, where radius r=1. The formulas simplify to:

  • x=sin(œï)cos(Œ∏)
  • y=sin(œï)sin(Œ∏)
  • z=cos(œï)

How the Correct Formulas Work üåê

The derivation of these formulas relies on a two-step application of basic trigonometry.

Imagine a point P on the unit sphere.

Finding the z-coordinate:

  • The polar angle œï is the angle between the positive z-axis and the line segment from the origin to point P.
  • This creates a right-angled triangle where the hypotenuse is the radius of the sphere (which is 1 for a unit sphere). The adjacent side to the angle œï is the vertical distance from the origin, which is the z-coordinate.
  • Using the definition of cosine (cos(œï)= hypotenuse/adjacent) we get:
    • cos(œï)= z/1 = z = cos(œï)

Finding the x and y-coordinates:

  • The side opposite to the angle œï is the radius of a circle created by projecting the point P onto the xy-plane. Let's call this projected radius r-xy
  • Using the definition of sine (sin(œï)= hypotenuse/opposite), we find the length of this projected radius:

sin(œï)= rxy/1 = rxy =sin(œï)

  • Now, we have a new right-angled triangle in the xy-plane. The hypotenuse of this triangle is rxy. The azimuthal angle Œ∏ is the angle between the positive x-axis and this projected radius rxy
  • Using sine and cosine on this new triangle:
  • x = rxy cos(Œ∏) = sin(œï)cos(Œ∏)
  • y = rxy sin(Œ∏) = sin(œï)sin(Œ∏)

By combining these two steps, we can find any point on a unit sphere using two angles.

Graphic Representation

How the Graphic Representation Works

The graphic representation of this concept, whether static or interactive, typically highlights two key right-angled triangles to demonstrate the formulas.

  • The Vertical Triangle üìê: This triangle is in a vertical plane that slices the sphere. Its vertices are:
    • The origin (0,0,0).
    • The point P on the surface of the sphere.
    • The point on the z-axis directly below or above P.
    • The hypotenuse of this triangle is the radius of the sphere, which is 1 for a unit sphere.
    • The adjacent side to the polar angle œï is the z-coordinate.
    • The opposite side to the angle œï is the projected radius onto the xy-plane.

This triangle is what gives us the formulas for the z-coordinate (z=cos(œï)) and the projected radius (rxy=sin(œï)).

  • The Horizontal Triangle üî∫: This triangle is in the xy-plane. Its vertices are:
  • The origin (0,0,0).
  • The point where the projected radius intersects the x-axis.
  • The point where the projected radius intersects the y-axis. The hypotenuse of this triangle is the projected radius from the vertical triangle (rxy).
  • The adjacent side to the azimuthal angle Œ∏ is the x-coordinate.
  • The opposite side to the angle Œ∏ is the y-coordinate.

Using this triangle, we get the formulas for the x and y coordinates: x=rxy cos(Œ∏) and y=r xy sin(Œ∏). Since we already know that rxy = sin(œï), we can substitute it in to get the final formulas: x=sin(œï)cos(Œ∏) and y=sin(œï)sin(Œ∏).
