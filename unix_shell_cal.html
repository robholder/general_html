Cal

Calendar

The cal (calendar) command is one of those classic, lightweight Unix utilities that has been built into the command line for decades. It is incredibly fast, simple, and surprisingly versatile once you know a few of its modifiers.

At its core, cal displays a formatted text calendar directly in your terminal. Here are the most useful ways you can leverage it in your day-to-day workflow.

1. The Basics: Checking the Present

If you just type the command by itself, it shows you the current month with today's date highlighted: 

cal

If you want to see a broader view of the current sequential period, you can view three months at once (the previous month, the current month, and the next month): 

cal -3

2. Temporal Time Travel (Looking at Specific Dates)

You don't have to stay in the present. cal allows you to inspect any month or year in history (or the future).

View a specific month of a specific year:

Pass the month (either as a number 1–12 or a name) followed by the year: 

cal 12 2026
cal december 2026

View an entire year at a glance:

Pass a single four-digit year to print a full 12-month calendar layout in your terminal windows: 

cal 2027

3. Advanced Utilities and Formats

Depending on your industry or workflow, standard calendar layouts might not fit your data tracking needs. cal has a few built-in structural toggles:

Display Julian Days (-j)

If you work in programming, astronomy, or logistics, you might need to know the Julian day (the sequential day of the year from 1 to 365). 

cal -j

This will output the current month, but instead of days 1–31, it will display numbers like 143, 144, 145.

Change the Start of the Week (-m or -N)

By default, cal starts the week on Sunday.

  • To force the calendar to start on Monday (the ISO standard):
    This doesn't work on macOS. 
cal -m

4. Creative Terminal Synergies

Because cal outputs plain text, it strings together beautifully with other command-line utilities.

Save a calendar to a text file

Need a quick reference calendar template inside a project folder? Pipe the output to a file: 

cal 2027 > 2027_schedule.txt

Pipe into grep to quickly check a layout

Want to quickly verify what day a specific date falls on without scanning the whole block? Combine it with grep highlighting: 

cal 07 2026 | grep "15"

On macOS, I had to use -N to flip the axis of the calendar layout: 

cal -N 07 2026 | grep "15"

A Fun Bit of History: The "Missing" Days of 1752

If you want to see a quirky software Easter egg that proves how historically accurate the Unix tool is, run this command: 

cal 09 1752

You will notice that September 1752 looks completely broken - it jumps straight from September 2nd to September 14th!

This isn't a bug. This was the exact month the British Empire (and its colonies) switched from the Julian calendar to the Gregorian calendar. To correct the cosmic drift in time, the King wiped 11 days completely out of existence. The cal tool accurately reflects this historical correction!

Man Utility

The absolute best way to figure out what commands are capable of on your current system is to ask the built-in manual pages.

The Manual: man

Type man followed by the command name to read its official, local documentation: 

man cal
  • Use the Arrow keys or Spacebar to scroll down.
  • Look for the "OPTIONS" section to see every flag allowed on your current system.
  • Type q to exit the manual and return to your prompt.

The Quick Help: -h or --help

Most utilities accept a help flag for a quick summary, though the older BSD tools on macOS can sometimes be strict about this: 

cal -h
# or 
cal --help

2. How to See the Default Environment Options

The behavior of commands like cal can change dynamically based on your Mac's global region settings. To see what defaults your system is using under the hood, use the locale command: 

locale

This prints your active environment variables. Look for LC_TIME. If it says something like "en_US.UTF-8", cal checks that file and defaults to starting the week on Sunday.

3. How to Start the Week on Monday on macOS

Because macOS uses the BSD version of cal, there is actually no command flag to cleanly switch the standard grid to start on Monday.

However, macOS includes a hidden sister utility compiled into the exact same file: ncal. It uses an alternative, vertical layout that natively supports a Monday start!

The ncal alternative:

Try running this in your macOS Terminal: 

ncal
  • The rows represent days of the week, and columns represent the weeks of the month, making it incredibly scannable for developer sprints! 
    May 2026
Mo     4 11 18 25
Tu     5 12 19 26
We     6 13 20 27
Th     7 14 21 28
Fr  1  8 15 22 29
Sa  2  9 16 23 30
Su  3 10 17 24 31

If you prefer the classic layout but want the extensive options of the GNU toolset, many developers use Homebrew to install the full package: brew install gcal.
