Challenge

One of the best ways to learn is to write your own code as often as possible, so here are three challenges for you to complete to experiment with your knowledge of GeometryReader.

First, put your ContentView back to the spinning color rows example we had: 

struct ContentView: View {
    let colors: [Color] = [.red, .green, .blue, .orange, .pink, .purple, .yellow]

    var body: some View {
        GeometryReader { fullView in
            ScrollView(.vertical) {
                ForEach(0..<50) { index in
                    GeometryReader { proxy in
                        Text("Row #\(index)")
                            .font(.title)
                            .frame(maxWidth: .infinity)
                            .background(colors[index % 7])
                            .rotation3DEffect(.degrees(proxy.frame(in: .global).minY - fullView.size.height / 2) / 5, axis: (x: 0, y: 1, z: 0))
                    }
                    .frame(height: 40)
                }
            }
        }
    }
}

With that done:

  • Make views near the top of the scroll view fade out to 0 opacity - I would suggest starting at about 200 points from the top.
  • Make views adjust their scale depending on their vertical position, with views near the bottom being large and views near the top being small. I would suggest going no smaller than 50% of the regular size.
  • For a real challenge make the views change color as you scroll. For the best effect, you should create colors using the Color(hue:saturation:brightness:) initializer, feeding in varying values for the hue.

Each of those will require a little trial and error from you to find values that work well. Regardless, you should use max() to handle the scaling so that views don’t go smaller than half their size, and use min() with the hue so that hue values don’t go beyond 1.0.

Ai Insight

QUESTION

What is he referring to when he says, "That three step layout system"?

ANSWER

He is referring to the foundational rulebook that governs every single pixel on a SwiftUI screen.

  • Propose
  • Choose
  • Position

Back in the second lesson you looked at, Paul listed them out briefly:

  • Parent proposes a size for the child.
  • The child uses that to determine or choose its own size.
  • Parent uses that to position the child appropriately.

While these three steps sound simple on paper, their ramifications - how they interact when you mix greedy views (like GeometryReader), rigid views (like fixed frames), and flexible layout tracks (like HStack) - are exactly why we had to use the "invisible box trick" to get your carousel working!

Let’s break down exactly what this conversation looks like under the hood so you can see the mental model Paul wants you to have.

The Three Steps in Action

Imagine a parent view (like your HStack) talking to a child view (like the 200×200 text card). Every layout happens as a strict, non-negotiable three-step conversation:

Step 1: The Parent Proposes

The parent looks at the screen real estate it has available and makes an offer to the child.

Parent view says: "Hey, I have a space that is 393 points wide and 852 points tall. How much of this do you want?"

Step 2: The Child Chooses

The child view looks at its own internal rules (its text length, image assets, or .frame modifiers) and decides its own size. The parent cannot force a size on the child; the child always has the final say.

Child view says: "I looked at my modifiers, and I am choosing to be exactly 200 points wide and 200 points tall."

Step 3: The Parent Positions

The parent accepts the child's chosen size, takes that box, and decides exactly where to place it within the available space.

Parent view says: "Got it. You are 200×200. Since my default rule is to center things, I am going to place you right in the middle of my coordinates."

Why this has major "ramifications"

The reason Paul emphasizes this is because beginners often think a modifier like .frame(width: 200) forces a view to change. In reality, modifiers are just layers of children.

When you write: 

Text("Hello")
    .frame(width: 200, height: 200)

...you are actually creating a parent-child relationship. The .frame is the parent. It measures the screen, and then it proposes a size to the Text. No matter what the screen offered, the .frame tells the Text: "Hey, I am proposing you take up 200×200."

Once you understand that layout is just an ongoing chain of Propose → Choose → Position traveling down your view hierarchy, weird layout bugs stop feeling like magic, and you can instantly see why you need certain frames or wrappers to get things to sit exactly where you want them!
