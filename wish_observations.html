Observations

Show Specific Issues

Bubbles Different in each shot

  • All elements arrive as procedural shader emissions (there are no 'lighter' added elements - no lighting added by Lighters), however:
    • Even when using the same Nuke scripts the bubbles look different in each shot
    • To compound this already 'not ideal' situation:
      • Every team treats these common procedural elements differently
  • I have kept an extensive prescriptive recipe (as it changes) to be able to render/composite/deliver bubbles, which is neither efficient nor consistent. This drains my time, and although I've been able to share my efforts amongst artist, this workflow is surely not desirable.
  • Key shots are constantly changed, developed and tweaked (even to the last week of production):
    • DLight - numerous MOD changes (incremental and spontaneous)
    • Nuke - each shot seems to demand unique and numerous changes EVEN to approach homogeneity
    • There doesn't seem to be any coordinated way of disseminating tweaks and changes, other than inefficient and accident-prone 'word of mouth'.
    • Either:
      • Look-dev should be in a reasonable status before implementation and/or
      • Scriptable updates should be considered (to assess a file and populate with changes/updates as needed

Continuity

There has been a lack of direction in keeping shots within the boundaries of continuity, proving challenging for temp artists:

  • As a 10 week artist, bringing "in between" shots to final has been hindered by goals that fall between disparate key-shots.
  • Continuity of techniques also hinders efficient delivery.

Production Coordination

For the first time at Disney, Shotgrid has served as a vague reflection of current circumstances, NOT as a prescriptive and informative resource:

  • Tasks are reported to coordinators by artists, rather than artists being directed by coordinators to achieve goals
  • Production (and Shotgrid) is following rather than projection, planning or serving as an accurate resource of current status.
  • I keep my own extensive planner to keep track of:
    • Other departments' status and expected delivery dates.
    • My own status per shot
    • Keeping accurate (and current) shot notes

General Issues

Shot Content Control

  • It seems that shots can be shown for review, yet obvious omissions or additions from upstream departments are often missed until way down the trail.
  • DLight seems to de-activate elements randomly, resulting in the archaeological technique of digging through upstream department publishes in DPix to compare results.
  • While a review of upstream departments' deliveries is obviously good practice, the disparity of 22 bubbles instead of the intended 24 bubbles seems to be inefficient and prone to preventable errors.
  • In the past, there were more department representatives watching for disparity at reviews.

SetExt

  • I had multiple conversations on multiple shots, involving me searching the server for file timestamps to establish why my setExt elements weren't correct, and multiple times for each shot.

FX

  • DLight seems to randomly set FX assets to a state of not 'activated' or 'do not render'.
  • Also, some FX elements are delivered to USD, even though they should be turned off in the final shot

Technical Workflow and Template Errors

Even though the Nuke scripts for WISH have been a remarkable achievement in Look-dev and a joy to use, there are still numerous technical errors and bad-practice that have created minor but ubiquitous flaws in the finished shots.

  • Bad edges:
    • Unpremult/Premult issues in the published templates:
      • Rules such as unpremult for color, premult for filters are not consistently observed
    • Premultiplied mattes being used with premultiplied images/layers:
      • The artifacts from these minor errors are visible in many shots, and I wonder if they have become 'accepted' since they are common.
    • Use of aliased Depth passes as color passes.

Lack of Automation

While there are many wonderful tools in use (and in permanent development and improvement), I feel there isn't enough engagement with automation (and a lack of cultural awareness of standardization for scripting). This would cover naming, formatting and tagging of created elements. Sometimes there is a sense of entropy, as established standards fall and erode.

  • Scripting could be embraced more (culturally) to accelerate and disseminate repetitive or precise changes.
  • When an artist-level scripts appear in ShadowBox, they seem to be used and embraced 'en masse', and on the whole save hundreds of hours with each publish, yet there doesn't seem a culture that values these additions. There is so much more that could be achieved in efficiency (time and aesthetically).
  • Scripts at artist level (as a complement to the solid pipeline) could (and should) be saving time and allowing for more refined and consistent results.

Email Subject Formatting (for Show/Seq/Shot):

This item perhaps illustrates a lack of understanding of the benefits of standardization.

Artists get countless emails, especially from Shotgrid:

With emails for every trigger or change for a shot, it is vital to have the ability to 'filter' emails accurately and consistantly. This makes each email a valuable resource, and provides an intuitive visual guide to what's going on, just by observing 'unread' emails as they arrive in shot specific folder (or Labels).

I remember on my first project experience at Disney, there were a few occasions when a Shotgrid email would appear that didn't comply to a common format for show/seq/shot etc. A friendly nudge via email or Slack message from a user would result in the format being refined or corrected.

This has been neglected over the years, and now even though I have my own automated filter creation script for GMail, I've largely given up trying to cover the numerous and random changes to the simple categories of show/seq/shot.

Too much unrefined email becomes a barrage of (ignored and useless) noise.
