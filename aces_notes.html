ACES

Terminology

  • HDR --> High Dynamic Range.
  • HDTV --> High-definition television.
  • UHDTV --> 4k Ultra-high definition television.

Color Space Examples

  • Rec.709 --> A limited color encoding specification - defined by the CRT televisions (cathode ray tube).
  • sRGB --> Also defined by CRT technology but for computer screens.
  • DCI-P3 --> Color encoding spec for projectors:
    • Digital Cinema Initiatives, LLC (a trade association of motion picture studios).
    • Wider gamut than Rec.709 and sRGB.
    • Can be reduced to fit television and computers (downstream)
  • Rec.2020 --> Updated color specification for UHDTV:
    • Wider gamut or range than DCI-P3

Capturing (Cameras)

  • Currently, cameras can capture more data than can be displayed:
    • Broader dynamic range
    • Wider color gamut
    • Most cameras use proprietary encoding
      • ACES provides a mechanism for reconciling them.

ACES - All The Eye Can See

Aim of ACES is to encode visual information regardless of hardware limits:

  • Every color the human eye can see
  • 30 camera stops of light

The color space is called ACES2065-1 ("ACES twenty-sixty-five, dash one").

  • Created in 2012
  • Created by the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences
  • Adopted by the Society of Motion Picture and Television Engineers (SMPTE)

IMP

ACES Archives are stored as an Interoperable Master Package (IMP), regardless of capture or origination.

Transforms

Digital data is transformed from one digital representation to others through various processes:

  • Video compression:
    • Reduces bits (for transmission or delivery)
  • LUT (Lookup Table):
    • Modifies the colors
  • ACES:
    • A family of transforms:
      • Transfer between color spaces
      • Uses a language called 'CTL' (Color Transformation Language)

Original Images

  • Original imagery is first transformed into ACES2065-1 color space.
  • Distribution imagery is transformed to match display targets:
    • ODTs (Output Display Transforms)
    • These are written for each output device or target.
    • Sometimes artistic tweaks are made to compensate for display shortcomings.

Types of Output Transforms

  • ACES2065-1 --> DCI-P3:
    • For theatrical releases
  • ACES2065-1 --> Rec.709 color space:
    • HDTV screens
  • ACES2065-1 --> SMPTE (ST 2084) color space:
    • HDR screens
  • ACES2065-1 --> Rec.2020 color space:
    • UHDTV screens

Viewing these on the 'wrong' displays will result in unsatisfying results.

Future Distribution

The gamuts and ranges of the capture devices is maintained in ACES, allowing for future developments and improvements.

More on Transforms

Output Transforms

The Output Transform (Not to be confused with the ODT transform)consists of three steps:

  1. An optional 'Look' transform:
    • Specific film stock emulation.
    • Bleach-bypass look.
  2. RRT (the Reference Rendering Transform)
    • Transform the image for a theoretical 'ideal' device:
    • Huge dynamic range.
    • Color space as vast as that of ACES2065-1.
    • This stores the image data in the 'master' form.
  3. ODT (the Output Display Transform):
    • Specific color space and dynamic range.
    • Tailored to the output device or target device.

Monitoring

The first interaction with ACES is in production; monitors, screens and displays.

ACES is the attempt to bring all these devices into a single common color space, and prepare the data for each output device and monitor.

Referencing the Real Scene

Much like a light meter approach (used with conventional film), ACES embraces the concept of the Reference Input Capture Device.

This looks at the scene and references the light and color visible to the human eye. This is called Scene-Referred Color.

  • Cameras are always a compromise:
    • ACES specifies how to use the limited capture data and put into the ACES color space.
    • CGI can produce more data than a physical camera, and this can be managed with ACES.

Perfect-Vision Monitoring

Just as there is the concept of a perfect capture device, there is also the concept of the perfect display.

ACES can prepare the data for this mathematical model, and from there enable the preparation of data for real-world display devices.
