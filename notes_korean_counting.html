Counters

Korean uses specific words called "counters" to define what you are counting. The counter is placed after the number.

Examples of Counters:

  • 개 (gae) - Used for counting general objects.
    • Three apples: 사과 세 개 (sagwa se gae)
  • 명 (myeong) - Used for counting people.
    • Two people: 두 명 (du myeong)
  • 마리 (mari) - Used for counting animals.
    • Four dogs: 개 네 마리 (gae ne mari)
  • 권 (gwon) - Used for counting books.
    • Five books: 책 다섯 권 (chaek daseot gwon)
  • 시 (si) - Used for counting hours.
    • Three o’clock: 세 시 (se si)
  • 분 (bun) - Used for counting minutes.
    • Fifteen minutes: 십오 분 (sibo bun)

By understanding these systems and counters, you can accurately count and refer to various items in Korean.

Why is the listed counting number for 3, 셋 (set), but in your counting example for apples, it's 세?

The difference arises from a grammatical rule in Korean where certain native Korean numbers change their form when used directly before a noun. This rule helps with smoother pronunciation and flow.

Here's a more detailed explanation:

Native Korean Numbers (Contraction Rule)

When native Korean numbers are used directly before a noun or a counter, some of them are contracted for ease of pronunciation:

  • 하나 (hana) becomes 한 (han)
  • 둘 (dul) becomes 두 (du)
  • 셋 (set) becomes 세 (se)
  • 넷 (net) becomes 네 (ne)
  • 스물 (seumul) becomes 스무 (seumu)

Example with Apples:

  • 셋 (set) - 3:
    • When counting generally or in isolation: 셋 (set)
    • When counting directly before a noun (e.g., apples): 세 (se)
    • So, three apples would be:
    • 사과 세 개 (sagwa se gae)

This contraction makes the phrase easier to say and more natural in conversation.
